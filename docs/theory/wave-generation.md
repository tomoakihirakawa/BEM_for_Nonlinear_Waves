# Wave Generation and Absorption

This page describes the wavemaker theories and wave absorption techniques used in the solver.

## Piston Wavemaker

A piston wavemaker moves horizontally with uniform displacement over the water depth. For a water depth $h$ and wavemaker stroke $S$, the generated wave height $H$ is related by the transfer function:

$$\frac{H}{S} = F(f, h),$$

where

$$F(f, h) = \frac{4\sinh^2(kh)}{2kh + \sinh(2kh)}.$$

Here $k$ is the wavenumber satisfying the dispersion relation $\omega^2 = gk\tanh(kh)$ for angular frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$.

In the BEM solver, the piston wavemaker is represented as a Neumann boundary whose normal velocity is prescribed as the wavemaker velocity. The solver does not impose a wave theory on the interior --- the nonlinear wave field develops naturally from the boundary motion.

## Flap Wavemaker (Schaffer 1996)

A flap (or hinged) wavemaker rotates about a pivot point at some depth below the still water level. The velocity distribution is no longer uniform over depth but varies linearly from zero at the hinge to a maximum at the surface.

Following Schaffer (1996), the transfer function for a flap wavemaker accounts for this depth-dependent motion profile. The second-order bound wave components can also be specified to suppress spurious free waves, improving the quality of the generated wave field.

As with the piston type, the flap wavemaker enters the BEM formulation as a prescribed normal velocity on the wavemaker boundary.

## Solitary Wave Generation (Goring 1979)

Solitary waves are generated by computing the required wavemaker trajectory from the target wave profile. Following Goring (1979), the depth-averaged velocity under a solitary wave of height $\eta$ traveling at celerity $c$ over depth $h$ is:

$$\bar{u} = \frac{c\,\eta}{h + \eta}.$$

The wavemaker displacement $X(t)$ is obtained by integrating this velocity:

$$\frac{dX}{dt} = \bar{u}\big|_{x = X(t)}.$$

The solitary wave profile is

$$\eta(x, t) = H\,\operatorname{sech}^2\!\left(\sqrt{\frac{3H}{4h^3}}(x - ct)\right),$$

with celerity $c = \sqrt{g(h + H)}$.

## Second-Order Wave Velocity Field

For regular waves, the velocity field can be specified to second order in wave steepness. The second-order Stokes expansion gives corrections to both the velocity potential and the surface elevation, reducing the generation of spurious free harmonics compared to a linear (first-order) specification.

The second-order terms are important when generating steep waves or when long simulation durations are required, as first-order wavemaker motion produces parasitic free waves that contaminate the wave field over time.

## Wave Absorber / Damping Region

At the downstream end of the computational domain (and sometimes upstream, behind the wavemaker), a wave absorber prevents reflections from corrupting the solution. The absorber works by adding artificial damping to the free-surface boundary conditions within a designated region.

In the damping zone, the dynamic and kinematic free-surface conditions are modified:

$$\frac{D\phi}{Dt} = -\frac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2 - gz - \nu(x)\,\phi,$$

$$\frac{Dz}{Dt} = \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial z} - \nu(x)\,(z - z_0),$$

where $\nu(x) \ge 0$ is a damping coefficient that ramps smoothly from zero at the inner edge of the absorber to a maximum at the domain boundary, and $z_0$ is the still water level. The damping strength and zone length are chosen so that waves are attenuated before reaching the boundary, typically over 1--2 wavelengths.

The absorber is purely numerical and does not affect the solution in the active wave region.
